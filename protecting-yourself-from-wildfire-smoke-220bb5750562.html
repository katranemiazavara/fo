Wildfire smoke is now a concern in many places. There are simple steps to take to protect yourself.

Filter, Filter, Filter

Filtration is the main tool to remove smoke from the air.

  • Run HEPA filters and use DIY Corsi-Rosenthal boxes in occupied spaces. Ensure proper air distribution when running them.
  • Upgrade the filter in your furnace to at least MERV-13 or MPR1900 and run the furnace continuously.
  • For commercial spaces, upgrade the filters in air handling units to MERV-13.

Ventilation is a Problem

Supplying outdoor air to the space is generally the main tool we use to control indoor air quality. When you replace indoor air with outdoor air, you are removing indoor air pollutants and supplying outdoor air pollutants. The vast majority of the time, outdoor air has a much lower concentration of pollutants than indoor air, so this approach works well. It doesn’t work during wildfire events. Ventilation should now be reduced:

  • Keep windows closed.
  • Turn off HRVs or ERVs that are connected to furnaces.
  • Close outdoor dampers on air handling units, recirculate the air and filter it instead.
  • Keep bathroom exhaust fans off as much as possible. They force outdoor air to infiltrate the building.
  • Still run the range hood while cooking, but try to limit the time it is on.

It’s important to note that eliminating ventilation is not a tool by itself to create a safe space. It should be combined with filtration to ensure there is a low concentration of smoke and other particulate matter in the space.

Avoid Going Outdoors

We should be making the indoors safe now to protect us from the outdoor air. Limiting time outdoors can prevent exposure to wildfire smoke. If you are going outdoors, wearing a respirator, like a N95, can prevent exposure. Please see the OSPE masking documents for more information.

Gaseous Pollutants and Activated Carbon Filters

There are gaseous pollutants that are still a problem and can be also increased because of wildfires. The main concerns are volatile organic compounds (VOCs), ozone and nitrogen dioxide.

The first tool is source control. Eliminate sources of these pollutants indoors.

Indoor air quality source control guidance from Health Canada. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/publications/healthy-living/infographic-improve-indoor-air-quality.html

Activated carbon filters can be used to reduce the concentration of these gaseous pollutants. In many portable air cleaners, these might not be effective as a long term strategy as they saturate and are not as well standardized as the particulate matter filters. Ventilation is usually used to control VOC levels, but since that is not an option now, activated carbon filters can help.
