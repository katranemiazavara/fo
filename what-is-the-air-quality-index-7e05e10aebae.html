You might have been hearing about the AQI lately. How does it work and what does it mean?

It starts with arbitrarily defining numbers based on a scale of good to hazardous.

Image from the EPA

The next step is picking pollutant concentrations that correspond to those levels. These are called breakpoints. For example, with wildfire smoke and general pollution, the main hazard is the fine particulate matter, also called PM2.5. PM2.5 concentration is measured in micrograms per meter cubed (µg/m³). The AQI breakpoints for PM2.5 are:

The AQI value for that pollutant is then calculated as a linear scale between the breakpoint values. For example, if the outdoor PM2.5 level is 45.5 µg/m³, that is half way through the 3rd category of “unhealthy for sensitive people” (half way between 35.5–55.4 µg/m³) . Since that category goes from 101–150, half way would be around 126, so the AQI for an outdoor PM2.5 concentration of 45.5 µg/m³ would be 126 .

Here’s a figure for what the values look like.

This can be done with other outdoor air pollutants like nitrogen dioxide and ozone. All the AQI values are calculated for the different pollutants and the largest one is used.

Air Quality Health Index

Canada uses a different scale. Its a formula that takes into account ozone, nitrogen dioxide and PM2.5 concentrations. The formula is:

AQHI = 96.1 x (e^(0.000537xO)-1) + (e^(0.000871xN)-1)+e^(0.000487xP)-1
Where O is ozone concentration in ppb
N is NO2 concentration in ppb
P is PM2.5 concentration in µg/m³

Assuming recent values of outdoor ozone (30 ppb) and NO2 (4 ppb), the AQHI scales almost linearly with PM2.5 concentrations.

The AQHI values as a function of PM2.5 assuming an ozone concentration of 30 ppb and a nitrogen dioxide concentration of 4 ppb (typical recent values).

This scale does not completely reflect reality because as one pollutant increases, it is likely that other pollutants will increase as well. For example, smog will have higher concentrations of both PM2.5 and ozone. The scale used is assuming ozone and nitrogen dioxide remain constant for the purposes of showing the relationship between PM2.5 and AQHI.

These AQHI values correspond to messages to the population.

What should the PM2.5 concentration be?

In the AQI, a PM2.5 concentration above 35.5 µg/m³ is when values start becoming unhealthy. The corresponding AQHI concentration is also around 35 µg/m³.

The EPA has set maximum exposure national standards in the US for a concentration of 35 µg/m³ as a daily average and 9.0–10.0 µg/m³ as an annual average.

The WHO has set a standard for maximum exposure of 15 µg/m³ as a daily average and 5 µg/m³ as an annual average.

The Public Health Agency of Canada has not set any standard, stating “indoor levels of PM2.5 should be kept as low as possible, as there is no apparent threshold for the health effects of PM2.5.” Creating a limit might imply that if you are below the limit, the exposure is safe, which is not the case for PM2.5. Therefore, this appears to be a reasonable position.

However, this position has had drastic consequences. Action is only required when limits are exceeded. In ventilation standards (ASHRAE 62.1.6.1.4.2), improved filtration is only required when the national limit of PM2.5 is exceeded. Since Canada has failed to set a limit, improved filtration is never required.

Overall, PM2.5 is a significant health hazard and the most harmful of airborne pollutants. It should be kept as low as possible through the use of high efficiency filtration.
